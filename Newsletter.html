Q1:
Please tell us about your previous experience(s) working as an Email Lifecycle Manager. What were you responsible for, and what are the projects/achievements that you are most proud of?

In every email role I’ve held, I’ve structured my lifecycle strategy around four core growth engines: Acquisition, Retention, Referrals, and Reactivation . This ARRR model has guided how I design flows, segment audiences, and measure success whether at Reliance Health , TurboTenant , Heystack , or WorldFront .

a. Acquisition : At Reliance Health, I developed an onboarding series that turned new signups into active users segmenting flows by plan type, location, and digital touchpoint. In another project at WorldFront, I supported acquisition by designing high-performing promo campaigns that converted first-time shoppers and aligned with seasonal buying behavior. In both cases, I focused on creating relevant first impressions that clearly communicated value.

b. Retention : This is where I’ve spent the most time and seen the greatest impact. At Reliance, I launched behavior-triggered email flows that encouraged ongoing clinic visits, pushed digital tool adoption on the mobile app, and personalized engagement based on user data. Similarly, at TurboTenant, I rebuilt lifecycle flows in Customer.io to maintain user interest through timely nudges  especially during long rental search cycles. Across all brands, my retention work has led to better engagement metrics and reduced drop-off across key funnel points.

c. Referrals : While not every brand had a mature referral program, I always looked for moments of high satisfaction to encourage organic sharing. At Reliance Health, I worked closely with the Customer Success team to embed referral prompts into post-NPS flows — these micro-campaigns felt timely and personal, and outperformed generic referral blasts. The key was always tying the ask to real sentiment, not just incentives.

d. Reactivation : Whether at Reliance, TurboTenant, or during my freelance work with Heystack, I’ve rebuilt winback flows that reached out to users who had gone inactive or disengaged. For example, after migrating TurboTenant’s email system from Klaviyo to Customer.io, I overhauled their reactivation campaigns using dynamic content tailored to past activity. This resulted in lower unsubscribe rates and a noticeable uptick in re-engagement clicks within the first 30 days.

Across all of these companies, I wasn’t just building email campaigns — I was designing intentional experiences that aligned with user behavior and business goals. I believe the inbox is an extension of the product, and every touchpoint should feel useful, relevant, and human.

Q2:
In your opinion, what makes a truly excellent lifecycle program? Feel free to share an example (yours or someone else’s) that really stood out to you.

A truly excellent lifecycle program is built on deep customer understanding, intentional timing, and continuous iteration using data. It's not about flooding inboxes, it’s about showing up with the right message at the exact moment someone is most likely to need it.

One of my favorite examples is from my time at Reliance Health. We built a multi-step post-signup series that segmented users based on their plan type, health goals, webapp and mobile app usage, and past engagement with digital touchpoints (like the symptom checker or appointment scheduling tool). Each user received context-aware nudges — some got preventive care prompts, others got support for chronic conditions, and some were reminded of underutilized benefits. Engagement rates doubled compared to our previous blanket-style welcome flows.

Another one: I’m always impressed by how Duolingo balances playful tone and serious reactivation logic. Their lifecycle program is a masterclass in user psychology nudging with humor, guilt, and gamification all at once.

In both cases, great lifecycle marketing didn’t feel like “marketing.” It felt like guidance.

Q4:
Please share a time when you realized that you might be wrong about something. What was the outcome? What did you learn? (Please use a professional example, if you have one.)

During an onboarding flow rebuild at Reliance Health, I initially assumed that longer email sequences (more touchpoints, more value)  would perform better. I designed a 7-step automation series packed with tips, benefit explanations, and product education.

But after reviewing early engagement data, we saw a steep drop-off after the third email. People were opening but not acting, we were overwhelming them.

Instead of doubling down, I paused the flow, met with the CX team, and ran a quick feedback survey. The insight? Our users didn’t want to be taught everything , they just wanted to be guided on what to do next.

We rebuilt the flow into a concise, 3-step journey with one action per email. Conversions (appointments booked) went up by 43%. That taught me a lasting lesson: even well-meaning communication can become noise if it’s not paced and prioritized.
